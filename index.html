The Question

Before touching any data, I ask: what decision is this analysis trying to support? In the NYC project, the question was not "what do the numbers say?" It was "should a new competitor enter this market, and if so, where first?" That framing changed everything, meaning I needed zone-level scoring, not platform averages.

Real Example: NYC Project

Initial brief: analyze ride-hailing economics in NYC.

Reframed as: four separate questions for four separate stakeholders: Uber CEO (margin health), Lyft CEO (expansion readiness), TLC Commissioner (driver compliance), New Entrant (launch viability).

The Method

I build the analysis around the decision, not around what the data makes easy to query. That usually means writing SQL that is harder to read but answers a more precise question. Aggregations, window functions, and joins are tools; the goal is always a number a stakeholder can act on.

Real Example: SQL Pattern

To find take rate by zone and identify outliers:

SELECT pickup_zone, AVG((base_fare - driver_pay) / NULLIF(base_fare,0)) AS take_rate, COUNT(*) AS trips FROM trips GROUP BY pickup_zone ORDER BY take_rate ASC;

The Finding

A good insight is not a summary; it is a surprise. The most valuable outputs from my analyses were not the ones that confirmed what stakeholders expected. They were the ones that challenged the framing. The R² of -0.08 for affordable homes in the housing model was more useful than the overall 0.79 because it told stakeholders exactly where not to trust the model.

Real Example: Housing Model

Expected finding: the model works well overall.

Actual finding: the model is worse than guessing for 50% of properties. The insight reshaped the deployment recommendation entirely: from "deploy broadly" to "deploy only above the $163K median."

The Output

Analysis that does not end in a recommendation is incomplete. I frame every project around what a business should do differently after seeing the data. This means writing for the person who did not run the SQL: the manager, the executive, the recruiter, not just the person who did.

Real Example: Recommendations

NYC project ended with four separate memos, each with a concrete action:

Uber: audit mid-fare pricing algorithm.
Lyft: exclude Staten Island Late Night from expansion targets.
TLC: investigate Brooklyn compliance patterns.
New entrant: launch LaGuardia first.

Your New Coworker Doesn’t Have a Pulse

January 2026 · 8 min read · Sourabh Akula

The Rise of the Silicon Workforce, and What It Means for the Rest of Us

I was mid-set on a bench press when it hit me. Not an injury. A thought. I’d been scrolling LinkedIn between sets (don’t judge me, we all do it), and three posts in a row were about the same thing. Companies “hiring” AI agents. Not hiring people to work with AI. Hiring the AI itself. Giving it a role, a title, a spot on the org chart.

I racked the bar, sat up, and just stared at my phone. Something about reading those posts in a gym, a place built entirely around human effort and discipline and sweat, made the contrast land harder than it probably should have. Machines were getting hired. And here I was, a data analyst with five years of experience, wondering if my resume was ATS-optimized enough.

That was October 2025. And over the next two months, every signal I picked up pointed to the same conclusion. AI wasn’t a tool in the toolbox anymore. It was sitting at the table.

The Research That Changed How I See This

McKinsey said we’re 18 to 24 months from agentic AI reaching full enterprise scale. Harvard Business Review called AI agents “a new category of talent” and told procurement departments to start treating them the way they treat staffing agencies. PwC argued the traditional pyramid-shaped org chart is dead. In the agentic era, one experienced professional can orchestrate teams of AI agents across an entire workflow. The word they used was “generalist.” Not specialist.

EY ran a survey: 84 percent of employees were eager to use agentic AI. In the same survey, 56 percent were worried about their own job security. Same people. Same breath. I felt that in my bones because I am both of those people.

The Numbers That Made the Fear Feel Rational

Challenger, Gray & Christmas reported that AI was cited as the reason for over 55,000 U.S. job cuts in 2025. Salesforce cut their customer service team from 9,000 to about 5,000 after AI agents started handling half their customer interactions. And then Dario Amodei, the CEO of Anthropic, wrote an essay calling AI a “general labor substitute for humans.” That’s not a fear-monger on Twitter. That’s the person building the thing.

A CEO from Calix said something I haven’t been able to stop thinking about: “80 percent of jobs will change 20 percent. 20 percent of jobs will change 80 percent.” Simple. Brutal. Probably true.

What I Actually Think

As someone who works in fraud detection, the things I do every day, writing SQL queries, building Power BI dashboards, flagging anomalies in claims data, are precisely the kind of multi-step analytical workflows that agentic AI is being trained to do. Some of what I do today will be automated within two years. I’m not guessing. I’m watching it happen.

But here’s what I keep coming back to. An AI agent can query a database. It can spot a pattern. It can generate a summary. What it cannot do is sit in a room with a fraud investigator who’s been doing this for 20 years and understand why that particular pattern matters in this particular context. That work gets more valuable when the grunt work disappears.

I had a moment in November that crystallized things. I used an AI agent to help build a SQL schema for my portfolio. What used to take me a full weekend was done in an evening. My first reaction wasn’t excitement. It was a weird, hollow feeling. If I can automate the part of my job I’m best known for, what am I actually good at? Eventually I realized: the fact that I could direct the agent to build something meaningful, that I knew what questions to ask, what edge cases mattered, what the output should look like before it was generated, that was the skill. The agent did the typing. I did the thinking.

The specialist era is ending. The person who can move from data extraction to insight to stakeholder communication while directing AI agents at each step, that’s who’s hard to replace. PwC called them generalists. I’d call them orchestrators.

Your new coworker doesn’t have a pulse. Doesn’t need coffee. Doesn’t have an ego. But it also doesn’t have your judgment. Your context. Your ability to walk into a room and make people trust a number. That’s still yours. For now, that’s enough. But “for now” has an expiration date. So move.

The Hardware Awakening

February 2026 · 7 min read · Sourabh Akula

When Atoms Finally Caught Up With Bits

I’ve been thinking a lot about something Jensen Huang said on stage in Las Vegas at the beginning of January. He was wearing his signature black leather jacket, and he said, almost casually, that ten trillion dollars of computing infrastructure from the last decade is now being modernized. Ten trillion. Like that’s a normal number to drop into a sentence.

But the thing that actually made me sit up wasn’t the dollar figure. It was the shift in language. He didn’t talk about software. He didn’t talk about models or prompts or chatbots. He talked about chips. Racks. Cooling systems. Physical machines. Jensen Huang stood on stage at CES 2026 and declared that AI was no longer a screen thing. It was becoming a world thing.

What CES 2026 Actually Was

CES 2026 felt different. The buzzword wasn’t “generative AI.” It was “Physical AI.” Intelligence being embedded into hardware, into robots, into vehicles, into glasses you wear on your face. NVIDIA launched the Rubin platform, basically a completely redesigned system of six chips that work together as one AI supercomputer. It uses four times fewer GPUs to train the same AI model compared to the previous generation, delivers ten times lower cost per token, and can be installed in five minutes. Microsoft, Amazon, Google, and Oracle all committed to deploying it in 2026.

The most jaw-dropping announcement came from Verge Motorcycles, a Finnish startup that showed up claiming to ship the world’s first production vehicle with solid-state batteries. Not a concept. A motorcycle you can buy for $29,900, with 370 miles of range, charging to 80 percent in about ten minutes, and rated for 100,000 charging cycles. Solid-state batteries have been “five years away” for about twenty years. And then this tiny company just goes ahead and does it.

The Bigger Picture

Most people are looking at these announcements as individual stories. A new chip here, a battery there, some glasses, a couple of robots. But there’s one story underneath all of them. AI is leaving the screen.

For the last three years, AI has been a software phenomenon. You type a prompt, you get a response. Everything happens behind glass. December 2025 and January 2026 were the months when that started to change. NVIDIA built the infrastructure to power AI in the physical world. Solid-state batteries are giving machines the energy density to run all day. Smart glasses are putting AI directly in your line of sight. Boston Dynamics brought its new Electric Atlas to CES, production-ready. Caterpillar partnered with NVIDIA to build AI-powered excavators.

What This Means for Data Work

I work in analytics. My entire career is built on the assumption that insights live on screens, in dashboards, in reports that someone opens on a Tuesday morning. But what happens when the screen dissolves? When the dashboard isn’t something you pull up on a laptop but something that floats in your peripheral vision while you walk through a warehouse or sit in a meeting?

The data doesn’t go away. If anything, there’s going to be more of it than ever. But the interface layer, the thing between the data and the human, is about to change so dramatically that the people who only know how to build static dashboards will find themselves building for a world that no longer exists.

A friend who works in product management said something that stuck: “The last ten years of tech were about making software smarter. The next ten are about making the world itself smarter.” The atoms are catching up with the bits.

A solid-state battery in a motorcycle might not seem like it has anything to do with your Power BI dashboard. But zoom out far enough and it’s the same story. The physical world is becoming intelligent. The people who can think across atoms and bits will define the next decade. The hardware woke up. And honestly, it was about time.

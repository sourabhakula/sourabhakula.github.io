Two Birds, One Tree

2026 9 min read

There is a story from the Mundaka Upanishad that has been living in my head for weeks now. I did not go looking for it. It found me, the way old wisdom tends to, at a moment when I needed it and did not know I needed it.

The story goes like this. Two birds sit on the same tree. They are close companions, bound together, beautiful of wing. One bird hops from branch to branch, eating the fruits of the tree. Some fruits are sweet. Some are bitter. But the bird keeps eating, restless, always reaching for the next one. The other bird sits near the top of the tree and does nothing. It does not eat. It does not hop. It just watches. Calm. Complete. Needing nothing.

The first bird is the jiva, the individual self, the ego. The part of you that chases, consumes, reacts, suffers, celebrates, suffers again. The second bird is the Atman, the true self. The part of you that has always been whole but that you rarely remember to look at. The Upanishad says that when the first bird, exhausted from tasting so many bitter fruits, finally looks up and sees the second bird in all its quiet glory, its sorrow falls away.

I read that on a Tuesday night in February, sitting on my couch after a long day, and something cracked open inside me.

What My Life Has Looked Like

I have been job searching. Actively. The kind where you wake up and the first thing you do is check your email for recruiter replies. The kind where you spend weekends rewriting resumes, tailoring cover letters, rebuilding portfolio projects, reading about what skills are in demand, what is declining, what the market wants. I have been tracking job descriptions in spreadsheets. Ranking them by fit. Optimizing my LinkedIn. Refreshing application portals.

At work, things have been fine. Not bad. But fine has a way of feeling insufficient when you are hungry for something more. The projects are steady. The people are good. But there is this low-grade hum underneath everything, this feeling that the world is accelerating around me and I am standing on a platform watching trains leave.

AI is transforming my industry. I know this intellectually and I feel it viscerally. I have written three entire blogs about it. I have researched the data, read the McKinsey reports, watched the CES keynotes. I know what is coming. And that knowledge, instead of calming me, has made the restlessness worse.

I have been hitting the gym harder than ever. Partly because it is the one place where effort and outcome are directly connected. You put the weight on the bar, you lift it or you do not. There is no ambiguity. No waiting for a callback. No wondering if your application made it past the ATS filter. Just iron, gravity, and your willingness to show up.

But even at the gym, my mind races. Between sets I am on my phone, scrolling job boards, reading articles, comparing myself to people who seem to have it figured out. And then the next set starts and for 45 seconds the noise stops. Just me and the bar. And then it is back.

That is the first bird. Hopping. Eating. Reaching. Tasting sweet and bitter fruits in rapid succession and never, not once, sitting still.

What a Friend Said Over Dinner

I had dinner with a close friend a couple of weeks ago. We were at a restaurant in Richardson, nothing fancy, just good food and the kind of conversation you can only have with someone who has known you long enough to skip the small talk.

I told him I felt stuck. Not stuck in a nothing is happening way. Stuck in a everything is happening and I cannot find my footing way. I told him about the job search, about the AI anxiety, about the weird dissonance of knowing more about an industry shift than most people and still feeling paralyzed by it. I told him I had been waking up at 3 AM with my brain running simulations of interviews I had not been called for.

He listened. He is good at that. And then he said something I have been carrying around like a stone in my pocket ever since.

"You know what your problem is? You are confusing motion with progress."

I did not say anything. I just sat there with it. Because he was right.

The Second Bird

The Mundaka Upanishad does not say the first bird is bad. This is important. The tradition is not asking you to stop eating fruit. It is not asking you to stop working, stop striving, stop engaging with the world. The tree is the body. The fruits are the experiences of living. You are supposed to eat. You are supposed to be here, tasting, reaching, living fully.

But the suffering comes when you forget the second bird exists. When you get so consumed by the eating that you lose contact with the part of yourself that does not need any fruit to be complete. The ego-self keeps hopping because it believes the next fruit will be the one that finally satisfies. The next job. The next salary bump. The next skill on the resume. The next interview that goes perfectly. And it never does satisfy, not permanently, because the hunger is not really about the fruit. It is about a deeper incompleteness that no external thing can fill.

I am not saying any of this from a place of having figured it out. I am saying it as someone who has been the first bird for months and is only now starting to glance upward.

I fasted last week. It was not for religious reasons, though the timing overlapped with something my family observes. I did it because I wanted to feel what it was like to not consume for a day. Not food, not content, not job listings, not LinkedIn, not news articles about AI. Just silence. Just sitting with myself on the tree.

It was uncomfortable. Really uncomfortable. By noon I was itchy, not hungry, but itchy. My fingers wanted to scroll. My brain wanted input. But I sat with it. And something quieted. Not permanently. But enough to remember that the second bird is still there, waiting, watching, complete.

I want to be that kind of professional. Not the loudest, not the fastest, but the one who understands. I am not there yet. Most days I am still the first bird, hopping and reaching and comparing. But the awareness that there is another way to sit on this tree, that there is a part of me that does not need the next fruit to feel whole, that awareness alone changes things.

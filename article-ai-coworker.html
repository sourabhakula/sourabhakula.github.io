Your New Coworker Doesn't Have a Pulse

January 2026 8 min read

I was mid-set on a bench press when it hit me. Not an injury. A thought. I had been scrolling LinkedIn between sets (do not judge me, we all do it), and three posts in a row were about the same thing. Companies hiring AI agents. Not hiring people to work with AI. Hiring the AI itself. Giving it a role, a title, a spot on the org chart.

I racked the bar, sat up, and just stared at my phone. Something about reading those posts in a gym, a place built entirely around human effort and discipline and sweat, made the contrast land harder than it probably should have. Machines were getting hired. And here I was, a data analyst with five years of experience, wondering if my resume was ATS-optimized enough.

That was October 2025. And over the next two months, every signal I picked up pointed to the same conclusion. AI was not a tool in the toolbox anymore. It was sitting at the table.

The Research That Changed How I See This

McKinsey said we are 18 to 24 months from agentic AI reaching full enterprise scale. Harvard Business Review called AI agents a new category of talent and told procurement departments to start treating them the way they treat staffing agencies. PwC argued the traditional pyramid-shaped org chart is dead. In the agentic era, one experienced professional can orchestrate teams of AI agents across an entire workflow. The word they used was generalist. Not specialist.

EY ran a survey: 84 percent of employees were eager to use agentic AI. In the same survey, 56 percent were worried about their own job security. Same people. Same breath. I felt that in my bones because I am both of those people.

The Numbers That Made the Fear Feel Rational

Challenger, Gray and Christmas reported that AI was cited as the reason for over 55,000 U.S. job cuts in 2025. Salesforce cut their customer service team from 9,000 to about 5,000 after AI agents started handling half their customer interactions. And then Dario Amodei, the CEO of Anthropic, wrote an essay calling AI a general labor substitute for humans. That is not a fear-monger on Twitter. That is the person building the thing.

A CEO from Calix said something I have not been able to stop thinking about: 80 percent of jobs will change 20 percent. 20 percent of jobs will change 80 percent. Simple. Brutal. Probably true.

What I Actually Think

As someone who works in fraud detection, the things I do every day, writing SQL queries, building Power BI dashboards, flagging anomalies in claims data, are precisely the kind of multi-step analytical workflows that agentic AI is being trained to do. Some of what I do today will be automated within two years. I am not guessing. I am watching it happen.

But here is what I keep coming back to. An AI agent can query a database. It can spot a pattern. It can generate a summary. What it cannot do is sit in a room with a fraud investigator who has been doing this for 20 years and understand why that particular pattern matters in this particular context. That work gets more valuable when the grunt work disappears.

I had a moment in November that crystallized things. I used an AI agent to help build a SQL schema for my portfolio. What used to take me a full weekend was done in an evening. My first reaction was not excitement. It was a weird, hollow feeling. If I can automate the part of my job I am best known for, what am I actually good at? Eventually I realized: the fact that I could direct the agent to build something meaningful, that I knew what questions to ask, what edge cases mattered, what the output should look like before it was generated, that was the skill. The agent did the typing. I did the thinking.

The specialist era is ending. The person who can move from data extraction to insight to stakeholder communication while directing AI agents at each step, that is who is hard to replace. PwC called them generalists. I would call them orchestrators.

Your new coworker does not have a pulse. Does not need coffee. Does not have an ego. But it also does not have your judgment. Your context. Your ability to walk into a room and make people trust a number. That is still yours. For now, that is enough. But for now has an expiration date. So move.

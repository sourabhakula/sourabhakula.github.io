The Problem

Can a simple second text message reduce clinic no-shows, and is the evidence strong enough to act on?

Working in disability insurance, I noticed claimants missing clinic appointments stayed on claim longer than those who showed up. I wanted to know if a simple second text message could change no-show rates at population level.

Modeled a mid-size FQHC (Federally Qualified Health Center) with primary care and behavioral health departments. Control group gets standard 48-hour reminder. Treatment gets that plus same-day 8am text.

The Insight

Primary care: significant. Behavioral health: underpowered. Same experiment, two very different conclusions.

Key Insight
"Underpowered is not the same as ineffective. The behavioral health result was not 'no effect'; it was 'we do not have enough data to tell.' That distinction changes the recommendation entirely."

The effect size was grounded in a Kaiser Permanente RCT across 76,577 mental health visits, providing real clinical evidence. Primary care showed clear statistical significance (p=0.04). But behavioral health was underpowered by a factor of 25, and the sample was too small to detect the expected effect.

This is the difference between "no effect" and "not enough data to tell."

The Solution

Deploy primary care. Extend the behavioral health pilot.

Statistical methods: Power analysis, two-proportion z-test, chi-square test, 95% confidence intervals, Cohen's H effect size. No-show rate dropped from 19.32% to 17.05% in primary care.

Expected annual revenue recovery of $61,000 against intervention cost of $211 in SMS fees. Business impact calculated across worst case, expected, and best case scenarios using CI bounds. Recommendation: deploy in primary care immediately, extend behavioral health pilot with larger sample.

What I Learned

  • Power analysis before the experiment saves you from misleading conclusions after. If we had not run power analysis, we might have concluded behavioral health reminders don't work. They might; we just did not have enough data.
  • Effect size matters more than p-value. A statistically significant result with tiny effect size is not worth deploying. The 2.27 percentage point reduction was both significant and practically meaningful.
  • Cost-benefit framing makes statistical results actionable. $61K recovery vs $211 cost is a ratio any administrator can act on. The statistics justify the decision; the economics justify the investment.

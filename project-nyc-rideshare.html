The Problem

How do you decide whether to enter a $5 billion ride-hailing market, and where to start?

The initial brief was to analyze ride-hailing economics in NYC. But that question was too vague to produce actionable insight. I reframed it into four separate questions for four separate stakeholders: Uber CEO (margin health), Lyft CEO (expansion readiness), TLC Commissioner (driver compliance), and a hypothetical new entrant (launch viability).

This reframing changed everything: it required zone-level scoring, not platform averages. Each stakeholder needed different metrics, different granularity, and different recommendations.

The Insight

45.9% of Uber's market segments are structurally loss-making. Lyft holds zero vulnerable zones.

Key Insight
"The ride-hailing market is not one market. It is dozens of micro-markets, each with different economics. Platform averages hide the zones that are bleeding money."

Uber's top decile generates 49.7% of all platform margin, an extreme concentration risk. Meanwhile Lyft's outer-borough strategy shows stronger take-rate consistency (63.4% solid trips vs Uber's 9.5%). LaGuardia Airport scored 100/100 as a new entrant launch zone. JFK scored 78.6.

The data revealed that the ride-hailing market is not one market; it is dozens of micro-markets, each with different economics.

The Solution

Four stakeholder memos, each with concrete action

Built 12 SQL query files against 100,000 TLC trip records (HV0003=Uber, HV0005=Lyft). Variables: fare, driver pay, wait time, zone, distance, violation flags. Created 4 Power BI dashboards, one per stakeholder perspective.

SELECT pickup_zone, AVG((base_fare - driver_pay) / NULLIF(base_fare, 0)) AS take_rate, COUNT(*) AS trips FROM trips GROUP BY pickup_zone ORDER BY take_rate ASC;

Recommendations:

  • 01
    Uber: Audit mid-fare pricing algorithm
  • 02
    Lyft: Defend outer boroughs before expanding; exclude Staten Island Late Night from expansion targets
  • 03
    TLC: Investigate Brooklyn compliance patterns
  • 04
    New entrant: Launch LaGuardia first, build dedicated WAV fleet in Bronx from day one

What I Learned

  • Reframing is the most underrated analytical skill. The initial brief would have produced a generic market summary. Splitting it into four stakeholder-specific questions produced four actionable memos.
  • Zone-level analysis reveals what averages hide. Platform-level metrics made both Uber and Lyft look healthy. Zone-level analysis revealed 45.9% of Uber's segments were bleeding money.
  • Build for the decision-maker, not the data. Each dashboard was designed for a specific person making a specific decision. That constraint made every chart more useful.

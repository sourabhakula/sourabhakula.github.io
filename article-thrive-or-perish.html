Thrive or Perish

2026 10 min read

I need to be honest about something. I have been writing these blogs about AI and the future of work, pulling in research from McKinsey and Deloitte and Harvard Business Review, sounding confident and analytical, and the whole time there has been a voice in the back of my head whispering a question I have not addressed directly.

What about me?

Not the abstract knowledge worker. Not the hypothetical mid-career data professional. Me. Sourabh. The guy who opens SQL Server Management Studio every morning, writes queries against claims databases, builds dashboards in Power BI, and presents findings to fraud investigators. The guy whose entire skill set overlaps uncomfortably with the capabilities of tools that are getting better every single month.

Will I thrive? Or will I perish?

What My Workday Used to Look Like

Here is what my typical workday looked like a year ago. I would get a request from a compliance lead or a fraud investigator. Something like, can you pull the claims data for this provider and flag anything unusual? I would spend a few hours writing SQL queries, cleaning the output, running some exploratory analysis, building a dashboard to visualize the patterns, and then I would write up a summary and walk them through it.

That process used to take me two to three days depending on the complexity. The SQL was manual. The cleaning was manual. The dashboard was built from scratch or adapted from a template I had used before. The summary was written in my own words after staring at the numbers until they told me something.

What It Looks Like Now

Here is what a similar request looks like now. I describe the question in natural language to an AI agent. It generates the first pass SQL query. I review it, adjust a few joins, fix an edge case it missed, and run it. The cleaning happens through a script that an AI helped me write months ago and that I have been iterating on since. The dashboard template already exists because I have systematized my most common layouts. And the summary? I still write that myself, because no model understands why a spike in short term disability claims from a specific region matters in the context of a fraud ring we have been tracking for six months.

What used to take two to three days now takes about four hours. That is not an exaggeration. And I am not even using the most advanced tools available.

When I first noticed this acceleration, I felt powerful. Like I had unlocked a cheat code. I was producing more, faster, and my work quality had not dropped. If anything, it improved, because I was spending less time on mechanics and more time on thinking.

Then, about two months in, the other feeling showed up. The one that keeps me company at 2 AM when I cannot sleep. If I can do my job in four hours instead of three days, what happens when my manager realizes that? What happens when the company realizes one analyst with AI tools can do the work of three analysts without them? That is not paranoia. That is arithmetic.

The Reframe That Stuck

I read an article recently that said something I cannot get out of my head. The real threat is not AI replacing your job. It is a peer who uses AI to do your job ten times faster.

That hit me because it reframes the whole question. It is not human versus machine. It is human with machine versus human without machine. And in that frame, the answer is not to resist the tools. It is to become so good with them that you are the person everyone else is trying to keep up with.

But there is a trap in that framing too. Because if the value proposition becomes pure speed, you are in a race you can never win. AI will always be faster. If your worth is measured in queries per hour or dashboards per week, you have already lost. The machine will always beat you on volume.

What I Can Do That the Machine Cannot

The machine can write a SQL query. But it does not know that Provider X has been flagged internally three times and that the compliance team has a complicated relationship with that account. I know that. The machine can build a chart. But it cannot read the room when I present the chart to a VP who does not want to hear what the data is saying. I can read that room and I can adjust my message in real time. The machine can generate a summary. But it cannot walk into a meeting with fraud investigators, listen to their gut instincts, cross reference those instincts against the data, and come back with a hypothesis that blends both. That is a human skill. My skill.

"The analysts who survive the next three years will be the ones who stop thinking of themselves as analysts."

A mentor told me over the phone last week: the analysts who survive the next three years will be the ones who stop thinking of themselves as analysts. The word analyst implies that your value is in the analysis itself. In the query. In the chart. In the numbers. But that is exactly the layer AI is eating. The analysts who thrive will be the ones who think of themselves as translators. People who stand between the data and the decisions. Between the model and the meeting. Between what the algorithm says and what the business should actually do about it.

What I Have Actually Started Doing

I automated my most repetitive weekly report. Completely. The one I used to spend every Monday morning on. It runs on its own now. The first Monday I did not have to do it, I felt useless. By the third Monday, I had used that time to sit in on a product team meeting I had never attended before. I learned more about how the business actually makes decisions in that one meeting than I had in six months of building dashboards.

I started asking my stakeholders different questions. Instead of what data do you need, I started asking what decision are you trying to make. It sounds subtle, but it changes everything. The first question makes me an order-taker. The second makes me a partner.

I have been reading about the regulatory landscape for AI in my industry, not because it is exciting, but because I realized that understanding compliance requirements around AI-driven decisions in insurance could make me indispensable in the next two years. Nobody else on my team is doing that. Nobody.

Thrive or Perish

I do not have a clean ending for this. I do not know if I will thrive. I hope I will. I am working like I will. But the honesty I owe you, and myself, is that I do not know. Nobody does. We are all running an experiment in real time, and the results are not in yet.

What I do know is that the analysts who perish will be the ones who refused to change. Who held onto their SQL windows like life rafts and did not notice the ocean had shifted. Who measured their worth in outputs instead of outcomes. Who let the machines do the thinking because they forgot that thinking was the whole point.

I refuse to be that person. Some days it is scary. Most days, honestly, it is scary. But scary and exciting live in the same neighborhood, and I would rather be in motion than standing still watching the world accelerate without me.

Thrive or perish. I choose thrive. Ask me in a year if I was right.

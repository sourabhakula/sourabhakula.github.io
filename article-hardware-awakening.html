The Hardware Awakening

February 2026 7 min read

I have been thinking a lot about something Jensen Huang said on stage in Las Vegas at the beginning of January. He was wearing his signature black leather jacket, and he said, almost casually, that ten trillion dollars of computing infrastructure from the last decade is now being modernized. Ten trillion. Like that is a normal number to drop into a sentence.

But the thing that actually made me sit up was not the dollar figure. It was the shift in language. He did not talk about software. He did not talk about models or prompts or chatbots. He talked about chips. Racks. Cooling systems. Physical machines. Jensen Huang stood on stage at CES 2026 and declared that AI was no longer a screen thing. It was becoming a world thing.

What CES 2026 Actually Was

CES 2026 felt different. The buzzword was not generative AI. It was Physical AI. Intelligence being embedded into hardware, into robots, into vehicles, into glasses you wear on your face. NVIDIA launched the Rubin platform, basically a completely redesigned system of six chips that work together as one AI supercomputer. It uses four times fewer GPUs to train the same AI model compared to the previous generation, delivers ten times lower cost per token, and can be installed in five minutes. Microsoft, Amazon, Google, and Oracle all committed to deploying it in 2026.

The most jaw-dropping announcement came from Verge Motorcycles, a Finnish startup that showed up claiming to ship the world's first production vehicle with solid-state batteries. Not a concept. A motorcycle you can buy for $29,900, with 370 miles of range, charging to 80 percent in about ten minutes, and rated for 100,000 charging cycles. Solid-state batteries have been five years away for about twenty years. And then this tiny company just goes ahead and does it.

The Bigger Picture

Most people are looking at these announcements as individual stories. A new chip here, a battery there, some glasses, a couple of robots. But there is one story underneath all of them. AI is leaving the screen.

For the last three years, AI has been a software phenomenon. You type a prompt, you get a response. Everything happens behind glass. December 2025 and January 2026 were the months when that started to change. NVIDIA built the infrastructure to power AI in the physical world. Solid-state batteries are giving machines the energy density to run all day. Smart glasses are putting AI directly in your line of sight. Boston Dynamics brought its new Electric Atlas to CES, production-ready. Caterpillar partnered with NVIDIA to build AI-powered excavators.

What This Means for Data Work

I work in analytics. My entire career is built on the assumption that insights live on screens, in dashboards, in reports that someone opens on a Tuesday morning. But what happens when the screen dissolves? When the dashboard is not something you pull up on a laptop but something that floats in your peripheral vision while you walk through a warehouse or sit in a meeting?

The data does not go away. If anything, there is going to be more of it than ever. But the interface layer, the thing between the data and the human, is about to change so dramatically that the people who only know how to build static dashboards will find themselves building for a world that no longer exists.

A friend who works in product management said something that stuck: the last ten years of tech were about making software smarter. The next ten are about making the world itself smarter. The atoms are catching up with the bits.

A solid-state battery in a motorcycle might not seem like it has anything to do with your Power BI dashboard. But zoom out far enough and it is the same story. The physical world is becoming intelligent. The people who can think across atoms and bits will define the next decade. The hardware woke up. And honestly, it was about time.
